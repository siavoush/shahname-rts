## Story

Rostam is the central [pahlavān](#) of the Shahnameh — a hero of superhuman strength whose deeds span the reigns of several Kayanian kings. Son of [[zal]] and born by a Simorgh-aided birth, he is raised in [[zabolestan]] and bonded for life to his peerless horse [[rakhsh]]. He performs the Seven Labors (*Haft Khān*) to rescue the foolish king Kay Kāvus from captivity, and for generations he is the shield of Iran against [[afrasiyab]] and Turan. Yet his greatest moments are tragic: he unknowingly kills his own son Sohrāb in single combat, and he is forced to slay the invulnerable prince [[esfandiyar]] — a killing that curses his own line. He dies at last by the treachery of his half-brother Shaghād, dragging his killer down with a final arrow even as he lies in a pit of spears.

## History

Scholars hypothesize — this is reconstruction, *not* Ferdowsi's claim — that Rostam and the whole Sistān hero-cycle derive from an eastern-Iranian, likely **Saka/Scythian**, epic stratum older than the royal Kayanian narrative into which Ferdowsi's sources wove it (Yarshater; Davis). On this reading Rostam is not a historical figure but a legendary one whose layer of the tradition predates the dynastic spine of the poem. The canon (what the text says) and the origin hypothesis (what scholars argue) are kept visibly separate here per `CONVENTIONS.md §6`.

## Primary text

> بران خستگیها بمالید پر / هم اندر زمان گشت با زیب و فر
>
> *barān khastegihā bemālid par / ham andar zamān gasht bā zib o farr*
>
> "She brushed her feather across the wounds — and at once he was restored to beauty and **farr**."

This couplet (verbatim from Ganjoor, *Rostam o Esfandiyār* §26) is an attested occurrence of [[farr]] in the text: gravely wounded and despairing against [[esfandiyar]], Rostam is healed by the [[simorgh]] and his glory restored. It is one excerpt of many to be added.

## Game lens

Rostam is the project's signature hero unit — the largest figure on the field with a glowing outline (per the placeholder-art spec). His arc is the natural home for the [[farr]] mechanic: a hero whose standing rises and falls with the justice of his actions, and whose lowest moments (the death of Sohrāb, the killing of [[esfandiyar]]) are exactly where the epic ties triumph to moral cost. Maps to `unit-rostam`.
