## Story

Jamshid is the fourth king of the Pishdadian dynasty — heir to Tahmuras the demon-binder, born of a line that has already gathered the arts of civilization. Ferdowsi gives him a reign of seven hundred years and casts it as a single arc: the apogee of human flourishing, then its collapse through a single sentence.

In the first five centuries he is a culture-bringer of staggering scope. He softens iron and invents armor, helmet, hauberk, and sword; he orders society into four classes (priests, warriors, husbandmen, craftsmen); he teaches spinning and weaving and dyeing; he draws gems from stone and perfumes from earth; he founds medicine; he builds ships. He then commands the *divs* to mix earth and water — the invention of brick — and from that flow palaces, public baths, and high vaulted halls. In the climax of his civic golden age he raises a throne so encrusted with jewels that, at his word, the divs lift it from the ground into the sky; he sits on it "like a sun in the air," and the assembled world showers him with gems. *That day, the verse says, they called the New Day* — and **Nowruz** enters the world. Three further centuries follow without death or sickness; the divs remain bound in service; the realm "saw nothing but goodness from the Creator."

Then the turn. Surveying his throne, *he looks across the whole earth and finds no one his equal* — and he speaks. He calls the elders, declares that no king the world has ever seen compares to him, that every art and every breath and every comfort flow from him, that none but he is sovereign. **The moment that word is spoken, the *farr* departs.** Ferdowsi marks the seam with deliberate brevity — *cho in gofte shod*, "when this was said." No battle, no apparatus. From that line forward the world fills with murmur, the day darkens, and into the vacuum walks [[zahhak]], the serpent-shouldered tyrant of the next age, whose thousand-year tyranny ends only when [[fereydun]] arises generations later to overthrow him.

## History

Jamshid is the Iranian reflex of the Indo-Iranian first king. His Avestan name is **Yima Xšaēta** — *Yima the Radiant* — the "shining" epithet that compresses through Middle Persian *Jam-shēd* into New Persian *Jamshid*. In the Avestan *Vendidad* he is given the golden **Vara**, a paradisal enclosure built against world-winter at Ahura Mazda's instruction; in the Vedic tradition the same Indo-Iranian root yields **Yama**, lord of the dead, suggesting that the deepest layer is a *first mortal* / first ancestor.

*Scholars hypothesize* (Christensen; Yarshater; Boyce — see *Encyclopaedia Iranica*, "Jamšid") that Ferdowsi's Jamshid is a layered synthesis: the Avestan first king + culture-bringer, the Indo-Iranian first mortal, and a Pahlavi (Sasanian-era) tradition that had *already* moralized the loss of *khvarrah* as a punishment for claiming divinity. The pride-fall arc, on that reading, is not Ferdowsi's invention but inherited.

*The text itself* makes one claim, and stakes the whole epic on it: a single declarative sentence — *"none but I is sovereign"* — costs Jamshid the [[farr]]. That is the political theology that runs through every later reign in the *Shahnameh*. Legitimacy is glory, glory is moral, and glory is *losable* — at the speed of a sentence.

## Primary text

The frontmatter carries three couplets from Ganjoor's Jamshid §1 (*Pādshāhi-ye Jamshid haftsad sāl būd*); the code session fetches them verbatim via `tooling/fetch-verse.mjs` and renders them here:

- **beyt 62 — the pride.** Ferdowsi names the moment: the god-knowing king grew proud and turned from God. The opening *mani kard…* is the lexical hinge; *mani* (selfhood, ego, "I-ness") is the named transgression.
- **beyt 70 — the moment the farr departs.** The brevity is the point: *cho in gofte shod*, "when this was said." No battle. No apparatus. The transgression is a sentence — Jamshid declares himself sovereign of his own making — and at that word the glory leaves him. This is the canonical farr-departure verse for the whole epic; it sits in [[farr]]'s entry as well.
- **beyt 74 — the dimming.** "Upon Jamshid the day grew dark," and the world-illuminating farr is waning. The arc closes within fifteen couplets of where it turned.

## Game lens

Jamshid is the archetype the [[farr]] mechanic is *built around*. He is not necessarily a playable unit in the RTS — but he is the *shape* every Farr-bearing hero must be able to enact: a high-stat curve sustained over a long horizon (the seven-hundred-year civic build) that collapses to zero in a single triggered moment of moral overreach, opening the door for the antagonist to enter the world. The mechanic should make that shape *possible* for any ruler — it must be possible to lose, in one act, what was built across many.

Concretely, the elements the Jamshid arc demands from the Farr system:

- A **gradient** — Jamshid's *farr* visibly *rises* across the build years; the meter is not boolean.
- A **declaration trigger** — a single act (decree, proclamation, hero ability) can collapse it instantly. The loss should *not* require an enemy intervention.
- A **vacuum effect** — collapse opens the way for an antagonist (the [[zahhak]] role); the system has a hook for "farr loss → enemy spawn or strength surge."
- **Reversibility only by another hero** — Jamshid himself never recovers; only [[fereydun]] does. The Farr is not a personal stat to grind back; it is a heritable mandate that has to be *retaken*.

When the Farr meter is implemented, Jamshid's arc is the **acceptance test** for it: if a designer cannot model "seven hundred years of just civilization undone in one sentence," the mechanic isn't capturing what the epic is about.
